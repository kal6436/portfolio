Keyrin Loves You

Game review and analysis by Kylie Lasky for DIGIT 100

This game was a delight to play, and I was glad to have chosen it for our review project. While it was short it kept me entertained with an interesting story being developed in a world with a gritty yet colorful palette. This review is broken into main categories following the assignment rubric.

Aesthetic enhancers:

The most stand out aesthetic piece of the game is definitely the artistic style, the low poly and pixelated style enhances an older vibe to the game which pairs well with the anime style chosen to depict the main fairy characters following styles of older anime titles, and the age of the technology depicted in the game ranging from a huge computer tower and monitors to floppy disks.

Settings:

Even though there were only 4 main settings they each had their own defining characteristics while all feeling connected to each other and the story.

  • The first area of the game, the dungeon, having brick walls and large chains scattered around paired with the whimsical background music sold the feeling of being in a fantasy world. Using winding paths to find fairies hidden within kept a mysterious feeling rising. After gaining more lore from the story and seeing there is a real world, it is easy to differentiate this area as being a digital world because of its fantastical creatures and objects of humongous scale.
  • As the player unlocks the shrine door of the dungeon we are brought to the real world which is much darker in tone. Workers are sitting on strike playing a gambling game, there are loud sounds of water rushing through pipes. Walking down a set of stairs we can see the disheveled sewage system the workers were working on. The music in the sewer area is static-y adding to the dreary vibe.
  • From the completion of a puzzle, we are allowed into the computer room. Though this room feels a bit out of place in the sewer due to it having a Japanese style sliding door for the entrance and not having any maintenance related items inside it seems like it was made just for the Doujin Triangle group instead of as a functional room for workers in the sewer. Inside is the perfect showcase of the personality for Kirin, the coder of the Doujin Triangle friend group, with bright bold colors, a large poster of the mascot to their game, and a huge old school computer connected to three clunky styled monitors. This room serves as the introduction to the Key-lo game and character by allowing the player to play it and unlock the final locked door.
  • Beyond the final locked door, we enter a very dark, wet almost cave-like area of the sewer. Though this dark scenery is quickly contrasted by Kirin’s appearance, her bright textures and especially hair make sure she is the focal point of this scene. The mystery of this scene is compounded by the music having an almost choir-like sound to it.

Objects (interactable):

Outside of settings the interactable objects within the game also enhance the aesthetic appearance by keeping an old-school style.

  • Translation patch: A floppy disk that is won as a reward for solving an argument between two non-playable characters. This patch is used to translate the Key-lo game from Japanese to English so the main player can play the game.
  • Computer: An old-school computer that fills up majority of the computer room with three large monitors displaying the Key-lo game developed by Kirin.
  • Tab organizer: Part of the main gameplay mechanic as a note taking device, its appearance resembles an old pager.

Challenges:

I absolutely love the main mechanic used in this game. The player character is given a note taking object openable with the tab key. It can be used to store up to 5 lines of key phrases which appear throughout dialogue interactions in fully capitalized text. Then the player is forced to repeat those lines in correct order to open locked shrine doors or create a poem to solve an argument. I would love to see more of this mechanic incorporated in the future development of the game.

The second mechanic that appears in the game is the dice gambling game, Key-lo. I had a lot of issues understanding the gameplay, as the game screen is heavily cluttered from a moving background, large changing numbers to indicate health, an updating text-box for moves being made, dice graphics showing the rolls, a count down in the center of the screen, and lastly small bold letters that appear to showcase numbers that were rolled… it is a lot to take it. Trying to learn the rules is also confusing, it is the first time the player has been allowed to use text input to open multiple windows so finding the rules was a little difficult, but when I had gotten into the menu the rules seemed to belong to a much more developed version of Key-lo, mentioning features they might not have been able to develop in time for this release version. If they continue to work on the development of Key-lo I would highly appreciate this design being simplified and made more friendly to players who do not know what this was intended to be. Also finding more ways to incorporate Key-lo in the story could be appreciated, but it also works as a one-time moment for the player to learn the origin of Key-lo.

Character player controls:

The player is kept to a single perspective throughout the entire game, never switching to another person. Though the player is walking through this world we do not get any real backstory to who we are, what we looked like, or why we are there, which leads to the user inputting themselves into the role. It is not until we are in the computer room and playing Key-lo do we get the option to insert a 3-character name for Key-lo to call us in the game. The experience is far more focused on the stories of the non-playable characters we interact with, which I enjoyed. I am super intrigued by the story that had been laid out so far in the game, though it feels a bit rushed and unfinished it was something I wanted to continue learning more about.

How could this game develop:

I have mentioned minor ways the game could develop so far, but here is my collected run down of improvement suggestions I would offer to the developers.

  • Cleaning up the design of the Key-lo interface, removing some of the constantly changing effects to more static ones with less color to make it easy for any user to digest and comprehend at a first glance.
  • Implementing ideas left in the rule menu of Key-lo would also help avoid the confusion of the current gameplay. It has a lot of potential to be a fun side game or main game piece but right now with the rules contradicting what the player is currently allowed to do, it takes a few playthroughs to understand what is important.
  • Continuing lore is the obvious next step for furthering the development of Keyrin Loves You. What is currently laid out sets up a lot of building blocks for intricate stories but there is little conclusions to any ideas brought up such as why are the workers on strike, what are the creature-like heads that can talk in the sewer, what else is Doujin Triangle up to, why is Kirin non-human, how has she lived and died multiple times?
  • Adding a skip for cut scenes would be a minor but helpful addition for people who are replaying the game. This option could unlock after the player has played the scene at least once before.
  • Lastly, adding chapter selections would be great once the story is further developed and could be divided into clear sections of story. As I was playing multiple times over, I started to get frustrated and disinterested because I was required to go through dialogue when I already memorized the key phrases when I just needed a screenshot from one section of the later game.
